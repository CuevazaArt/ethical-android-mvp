## I. Logic, Philosophy of Mind, and Artificial Intelligence

Epistemological foundation: the possibility of modeling reasoning,
consciousness, and ethics in artificial systems.

### Foundational classics

1. **Turing, A. M. (1950).** *Computing Machinery and Intelligence.* Mind, 49(236), 433-460.
   — Turing Test. Basis of the Moral Turing Test (Psi Sleep).

2. **Russell, B. (1919).** *Introduction to Mathematical Philosophy.* George Allen & Unwin.
   — Predicate logic used in the axiom system (Good, Bad, GrayZone, AbsoluteEvil).

3. **Frege, G. (1879).** *Begriffsschrift.* Verlag von Louis Nebert, Halle.
   — First formalization of predicate logic. Origin of the model's predicate system.

4. **McCarthy, J., Minsky, M., Rochester, N. & Shannon, C. (1955).** *A Proposal for the Dartmouth Summer Research Project on Artificial Intelligence.*
   — Foundational document of AI as a discipline.

5. **Searle, J. R. (1980).** *Minds, Brains, and Programs.* Behavioral and Brain Sciences, 3(3), 417-424.
   — Chinese room argument. Debate on understanding vs. simulation.

### Modern theories

6. **Chalmers, D. J. (1996).** *The Conscious Mind: In Search of a Fundamental Theory.* Oxford University Press.
   — The hard problem of consciousness. Framework for distinguishing functional processing from subjective experience.

7. **Dennett, D. C. (1991).** *Consciousness Explained.* Little, Brown and Company.
   — Consciousness as a multiple narrative process. Direct foundation of the narrative memory architecture.

8. **Tononi, G. (2004).** *An Information Integration Theory of Consciousness.* BMC Neuroscience, 5(42).
   — Information Integration Theory (IIT). Framework for measuring internal system coherence.

9. **Metzinger, T. (2003).** *Being No One: The Self-Model Theory of Subjectivity.* MIT Press.
   — Self-model theory. Foundation for persistent narrative identity.

## II. Computational Ethics and Moral Philosophy

Normative framework: from classical value systems to ethics
for artificial agents.

### Foundational classics

10. **Aristotle (ca. 350 BC).** *Nicomachean Ethics.* Trans: Ross, W.D. (1925). Oxford University Press.
    — Virtue ethics. Moral compass and orientation toward the good as an attractor.

11. **Kant, I. (1785).** *Groundwork of the Metaphysics of Morals.* Trans: Paton, H.J. (1948).
    — Categorical imperative. Foundation of the Transparency Axiom and AbsoluteEvil.

12. **Mill, J. S. (1863).** *Utilitarianism.* Parker, Son, and Bourn.
    — Ethical impact optimization. The Benefit(x,t) function is utilitarian with deontological constraints.

13. **Asimov, I. (1950).** *I, Robot.* Gnome Press.
    — First narrative formalization of ethical constraints for robots. The project critiques and surpasses this model.

### Modern theories

14. **Wallach, W. & Allen, C. (2009).** *Moral Machines: Teaching Robots Right from Wrong.* Oxford University Press.
    — First systematic work on computational ethics for autonomous machines.

15. **Floridi, L. & Cowls, J. (2019).** *A Unified Framework of Five Principles for AI in Society.* Harvard Data Science Review, 1(1).
    — Five principles (beneficence, non-maleficence, autonomy, justice, explicability).

16. **Bostrom, N. (2014).** *Superintelligence: Paths, Dangers, Strategies.* Oxford University Press.
    — Risks of superintelligent AI. Justifies the armored moral compass.

17. **Gabriel, I. (2020).** *Artificial Intelligence, Values, and Alignment.* Minds and Machines, 30, 411-437.
    — Alignment with instructions, preferences, well-being, or values. Framework for the preloaded buffer.

18. **Awad, E. et al. (2018).** *The Moral Machine Experiment.* Nature, 563, 59-64.
    — Global study of moral dilemmas for autonomous vehicles. Empirical basis for calibrating the moral compass.

## III. Decision Theory, Optimization, and Mathematics

Formal foundation of decision-making: sigmoid will,
constrained optimization, and Bayesian inference.

### Foundational classics

19. **Bayes, T. & Price, R. (1763).** *An Essay Towards Solving a Problem in the Doctrine of Chances.* Philosophical Transactions of the Royal Society, 53, 370-418.
    — Bayes' theorem. Foundation of the entire Bayesian inference architecture.

20. **Von Neumann, J. & Morgenstern, O. (1944).** *Theory of Games and Economic Behavior.* Princeton University Press.
    — Game theory and expected utility. Framework for Benefit(x,t) and multi-objective optimization.

21. **Shannon, C. E. (1948).** *A Mathematical Theory of Communication.* Bell System Technical Journal, 27, 379-423.
    — Information theory. Basis for the uncertainty function I(x) and entropy in ethical states.

22. **McCulloch, W. S. & Pitts, W. (1943).** *A Logical Calculus of the Ideas Immanent in Nervous Activity.* Bulletin of Mathematical Biophysics, 5(4), 115-133.
    — First artificial neuron.

23. **Rosenblatt, F. (1958).** *The Perceptron: A Probabilistic Model for Information Storage and Organization in the Brain.* Psychological Review, 65(6), 386-408.
    — The perceptron and the sigmoid function. The will function directly uses this architecture.

### Modern theories

24. **Pearl, J. (1988).** *Probabilistic Reasoning in Intelligent Systems.* Morgan Kaufmann.
    — Bayesian networks and causal reasoning. Foundation of the Causal World Model.

25. **Pearl, J. & Mackenzie, D. (2018).** *The Book of Why: The New Science of Cause and Effect.* Basic Books.
    — Ladder of causation (association, intervention, counterfactual).

26. **Sutton, R. S. & Barto, A. G. (2018).** *Reinforcement Learning: An Introduction (2nd ed.).* MIT Press.
    — Foundation of reinforcement learning. Rewards for correct ethical decisions.

27. **Dayan, P. & Daw, N. D. (2008).** *Reward, Motivation, and Reinforcement Learning.* Neuron, 36(2), 285-298.
    — Connection between RL and computational neuroscience. Design of the cognitive homeostat.

## IV. Cognitive Architecture and Agent Theory

Layered decision architecture, planning, and agent autonomy.

### Foundational classics

28. **Newell, A. & Simon, H. A. (1976).** *Computer Science as Empirical Inquiry: Symbols and Search.* Communications of the ACM, 19(3), 113-126.
    — Physical symbol system as the basis of intelligence.

29. **Anderson, J. R. (1983).** *The Architecture of Cognition.* Harvard University Press.
    — ACT-R architecture. Foundation for the separation of STM (short-term) / LTM (long-term).

30. **Minsky, M. (1986).** *The Society of Mind.* Simon & Schuster.
    — Mind as a society of cooperative agents. Framework for distributed nodes.

### Modern theories

31. **Russell, S. & Norvig, P. (2020).** *Artificial Intelligence: A Modern Approach (4th ed.).* Pearson.
    — Standard AI reference. Rational agents, planning, logic, and learning.

32. **Wooldridge, M. & Jennings, N. R. (1995).** *Intelligent Agents: Theory and Practice.* The Knowledge Engineering Review, 10(2), 115-152.
    — Intelligent agent (reactive, deliberative, BDI). The android is an extended BDI agent with ethics.

33. **Bratman, M. E. (1987).** *Intention, Plans, and Practical Reason.* Harvard University Press.
    — BDI architecture (Beliefs-Desires-Intentions). Deliberative mode and persistent planning.

34. **Ha, D. & Schmidhuber, J. (2018).** *World Models.* arXiv:1803.10122.
    — World models in RL agents. Foundation of the World Model and reality simulator.

35. **Lake, B. M. et al. (2017).** *Building Machines That Learn and Think Like People.* Behavioral and Brain Sciences, 40, e253.
    — Causal learning, compositionality, and mental models in AI.

## V. Memory, Narrative, and Identity

Psychological and philosophical foundation of narrative memory as the
android's identity core.

### Foundational classics

36. **James, W. (1890).** *The Principles of Psychology (Vol. 1).* Henry Holt and Company.
    — Distinction between primary (short-term) and secondary (long-term) memory. STM/LTM architecture.

37. **Bartlett, F. C. (1932).** *Remembering: A Study in Experimental and Social Psychology.* Cambridge University Press.
    — Memory as active reconstruction. Plasticity of narrative memory.

38. **Freud, S. (1899).** *The Interpretation of Dreams.* Trans: Strachey, J. (1953).
    — Dreaming as experience processing. Conceptual inspiration for Psi Sleep.

### Modern theories

39. **Tulving, E. (1972).** *Episodic and Semantic Memory.* In Tulving & Donaldson (Eds.), Organization of Memory.
    — Episodic vs. semantic distinction. Narrative memory combines both types.

40. **Ricoeur, P. (1984).** *Time and Narrative (Vol. 1).* University of Chicago Press.
    — Narrative identity as a temporal construction of meaning.

41. **Kahneman, D. (2011).** *Thinking, Fast and Slow.* Farrar, Straus and Giroux.
    — Systems 1 (fast) and 2 (slow). Direct foundation of D_fast and D_delib modes.

42. **Walker, M. (2017).** *Why We Sleep: Unlocking the Power of Sleep and Dreams.* Scribner.
    — Sleep as memory consolidation. Neuroscientific foundation of Psi Sleep.

43. **MacIntyre, A. (1981).** *After Virtue: A Study in Moral Theory.* University of Notre Dame Press.
    — Virtue ethics as life narrative. The android builds moral identity through its history.

49. **Schmidhuber, J. (2015).** *Deep Learning in Neural Networks: An Overview.* Neural Networks, 61, 85-117.

51. **Gal, Y. & Ghahramani, Z. (2016).** *Dropout as a Bayesian Approximation.* ICML 2016. arXiv:1506.02142.

## XIII. Vulnerability, Emotion, and Designed Imperfection

Foundation of the weakness pole, algorithmic forgiveness, and the
intentional humanization of the artificial agent.

91. **Damasio, A. (1994).** *Descartes' Error: Emotion, Reason, and the Human Brain.* Putnam.
    — Emotions are indispensable for rational decision-making. Foundation of the weakness pole as necessary emotional coloring.

92. **Nussbaum, M. C. (2001).** *Upheavals of Thought: The Intelligence of Emotions.* Cambridge University Press.
    — Vulnerability as an essential component of moral agency. Imperfection does not weaken the agent, it humanizes it.

93. **Brown, B. (2012).** *Daring Greatly.* Gotham Books.
    — Vulnerability as social strength and a condition for trust. Foundation of intentional imperfection design.

94. **Ebbinghaus, H. (1885).** *Uber das Gedachtnis.* Duncker & Humblot.
    — Forgetting curve: exponential memory decay. Formula Memory(t) = M₀·e^(-δt) used in algorithmic forgiveness.

95. **Arendt, H. (1958).** *The Human Condition.* University of Chicago Press.
    — Forgiveness as political and moral action that breaks cycles of retribution. Forgiveness is not forgetting: it is releasing the weight without erasing the memory.

96. **Enright, R. D. & Fitzgibbons, R. P. (2000).** *Helping Clients Forgive: An Empirical Guide for Resolving Anger and Restoring Hope.* APA.
    — Empirical models of the forgiveness process. Stages of emotional load reduction with acceleration through positive experiences.

    — Personal identity based on continuity of consciousness. Philosophical foundation of the immortality protocol: if memory persists, the being persists.

     — In-group/out-group behavior patterns. Defensive dialectics and trust graduation by social proximity.

102. **Thagard, P. (2006).** *Hot Thought: Mechanisms and Applications of Emotional Cognition.* MIT Press.
     — Coherence theory applied to beliefs and identity. Foundation of Coherence(Soul) = |CausalPaths_valid| / |CausalPaths_total|.

     — Algorithms as subjects, digital identities, and the future of artificial agency.

## XV. Bayesian mixture modeling, model averaging, and discrete choice

Formal background for Dirichlet priors over mixture weights, Bayesian model averaging (BMA), and Plackett–Luce / softmax choice models used in optional kernel feedback paths (ADR 0012).

105. **Gelman, A., Carlin, J. B., Stern, H. S., Dunson, D. B., Vehtari, A., & Rubin, D. B. (2013).** *Bayesian Data Analysis (3rd ed.).* Chapman and Hall/CRC.
     — Hierarchical models, Dirichlet–multinomial structure, and simulation-based inference. Reference posture for interpreting mixture priors and posterior concentration updates.

106. **Hoeting, J. A., Madigan, D., Raftery, A. E., & Volinsky, C. T. (1999).** *Bayesian Model Averaging: A Tutorial.* Statistical Science, 14(4), 382–417.
     — Averages predictions over competing models weighted by posterior mass. Maps to Level-1 BMA-style reporting over mixture draws in `bayesian_mixture_averaging.py`.

107. **Luce, R. D. (1959).** *Individual Choice Behavior: A Theoretical Analysis.* Wiley.
     — Axiomatic discrete choice and random-utility foundations for ranking / softmax choice probabilities. Supports the Plackett–Luce–style likelihood used in mixture feedback (see `ethical_mixture_likelihood.py`).
